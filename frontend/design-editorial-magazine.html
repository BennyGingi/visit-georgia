The Oldest Wine
in the World Poured
Here First

Long before France had a chateau and Italy had a vineyard, the people of the South Caucasus were fermenting Rkatsiteli grapes in terracotta vessels buried underground. What they created was not simply wine — it was civilisation.

In the village of Sighnaghi, perched above the Alazani plain in the Kakheti region, the rhythm of life has not changed much in three thousand years. Come harvest season — known as Rtveli — the entire community descends on the vineyards. Grapes are trodden by foot. The must, skins, seeds, and stems are shovelled into great clay amphorae called qvevri, sealed with beeswax, and buried in the cool earth of a cellar called a marani. There the wine sleeps through winter, fermenting with wild yeasts in a process that predates written language.

The result is unlike anything produced by modern oenology. Amber wines — known to the outside world as "orange wines" — emerge from the qvevri with a golden hue, tannins usually reserved for red wine, and a complexity that has intoxicated drinkers from Tbilisi to Tokyo. The method was awarded UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage status in 2013, confirming what Georgians have always known: this is not agriculture. It is heritage carried in the blood.

Today, Kakheti accounts for more than 70 percent of Georgia's wine output and draws a new generation of travellers who swap the Loire Valley for the Alazani, and the Barossa for the Kartli. Boutique guesthouses and family-run wineries offer immersive experiences — from grape picking to qvevri sealing — that no conventional wine tour can replicate.

“

Wine is not a product for us. It is a prayer. Every qvevri buried in the earth is a conversation between the living and the dead.

Giorgi Dakishvili  —  Third-generation vintner, Telavi

The region's hospitality tradition — Tamada, the art of the toast-master at a feast — is equally profound. The Supra, a ceremonial Georgian feast, is a performance of memory, belonging, and abundance. Dishes arrive without end: churchkhela (walnut-and-grape confections), khinkali dumplings heavy with broth, pkhali herb salads, and whole suckling pigs glazed with pomegranate. The Tamada rises, proposes a toast to those absent, and the qvevri wine flows into horn-shaped drinking vessels. You do not simply eat at a Supra. You participate in something ancient.

Four Experiences
That Define Georgia

Georgia resists simple categorisation. It is simultaneously a mountain kingdom, a wine cradle, a living church-art museum, and one of the world's great culinary cultures. Here are the four encounters that every traveller takes home.

⛰

Mountain Kingdom

The Greater Caucasus holds peaks higher than the Alps. Drive the Georgian Military Highway to Kazbegi, where the 14th-century Gergeti Trinity Church sits above the clouds. In winter, Gudauri offers world-class heliskiing without the crowds of the Alps.

Plan a mountain trip  →
🍷

Ancient Wine

Join a Rtveli grape harvest in Kakheti. Tread grapes by foot, seal a qvevri with beeswax, attend a Supra feast presided over by a Tamada. Georgia's wine culture is not a tourist attraction — it is an invitation into the country's living soul.

Explore wine country  →
🎸

Living Culture

Georgian polyphonic singing — also UNESCO-listed — fills churches and town squares with a haunting three-part harmony that exists nowhere else on earth. Attend a performance of the Rustavi Choir, visit cave city Uplistsikhe, or explore Tbilisi's National Museum.

Discover Georgian culture  →
🍲

Extraordinary Food

Khinkali dumplings, khachapuri cheese bread, churchkhela grape-and-walnut confections, walnut-stuffed badrijani aubergine — Georgian cuisine is intensely regional, absurdly fresh, and deeply generous. No visitor leaves hungry. Most leave unable to stop thinking about it.
